# Quantifying Political Momentum with Data

Political commentators get paid to talk about the current political landscape every day, and while watching these talking heads do their thing on TV, I always hear the words “Political Momentum” over and over again. This was especially the case after the 2016 election and is true again after the 2018 election. But what is political Momentum? Both Republicans and Democrats claimed to have it after the 2016 election. How can this be when someone won and someone lost? I started thinking of ways to be able to quantify political momentum; Bills past, approval ratings from polls, and other polling information all came to mind but the problem with all of those is that they can all be skewed by people and most of the time they only account for people who have voted in the past. Scrolling through the Colorado Secretaries of State’s website I came across voter registration and thought this was a great way to be able to measure political momentum. If people like a parties message they switch their party affiliation and when new people register to vote they can choose a party affiliation as well. This will allow me to see if either people are changing their party affiliation or registering with a party affiliation. With the way, I collected my data I can not tell which but either it will let me know if a political party is gaining momentum by counting their party size. This is going to be my way to quantify “Political Momentum.” Also, to be able to try out some more data science techniques, I tried to forecast the 2018 voter registration counts as well.

All my data was downloaded from the Colorado Secretaries of State’s website. One thing that is great about working with political data is that most of it is considered public information and can be downloaded for free. An example in which you have to pay “administrative fees” to get public data is the voter registration file. This shows information about every registered voter in the state and gets a little pricey for normal citizen due to its size. Very interesting file if you ever get your hands on it though. But, I am lucky and I was able to download my information for free. What I downloaded was the Voter Registration Statistics excel file. This file contains information about the number of registered voters for the month it was released. Some examples sheets in this file are; Party and Status, Gender Party and Age, as well as breakdowns by Congressional districts, State Senate districts, State House districts, and by Precincts. The information that I wanted was from the Party and Status sheet. Below is what the Party and Status sheet generally looks like.

![data_preview](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\data_preview.png)

I ended up downloading 60 of these files. One for each month from Nov 2012 to Oct 2017. As easy at it may sound to just write a script to load all of these files into a format that is usable inside python, there were a few problems with the files themselves. For one, not all of the years had the same formate. In the years before 2015(2012–2014) there were no “Prereg” voters as seen above, the “UNI” (Unity) party was replaced with the AEL (Americans Elect) party and in a different spot at seen above while it was an older version of excel as well. What I did not know is that Pandas reads .xls and .xlsx files differently while upgrading the file format was not hard, it was a tedious task. All of the issues above were fixed by using excel but I do wish the Colorado Secretary of State’s website was more data geek-friendly.

Some of the changes I made to the data to simplify my analysis was to add all “Prereg” voters to active voters. “Prereg” voters are people to register to vote but not actually old enough yet. An example would be when a 17-year-old registers to vote before their 18th birthday but will be able to vote in the next election because they will be 18 by then. I could not find out where these “Prereg” voters were counted before 2015 so I added them to active voters because I felt it helped provide a look into new and young voters. I also added all the political parties besides Democrats and Republicans to the Unaffiliated counts. This was to simplify my analysis and only having to focus on three variables instead of seven.

To start off I wanted to be able to familiarize my self with the data, and the easiest way to do that was to graph the data. I first started to look at total registered voters for all counties and active voters by political parties for all counties. Both of these graphs would give me a great overview of all the data I was working with.

![total_voters](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\total_voters_all.png)

Above are total registered voters across all counties and parties. Some of the key interest points from this graph shows that the state of Colorado cleans its voter rolls after even-year elections. This means removing people that have died or moved away and not voted in a number of years. Another interesting point is the increase in voter registrations right before even year elections but the same increase does not happen before odd-year elections. The last thing I would like to point out about this graph is, because of it patterned increases and decreases it is confident to say that this graph has a seasonality of 2 year or 24 months. This will be important when it comes to forecasting out future registration numbers.

![active_voters](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\active_voters_all.png)

Above is a graph of all active voters by political parties for all counties. This graph was very informative and really provided some good insights into voter registration rates across Colorado. The first thing I noticed was the huge spike that happened in the year 2013 across all active voters but was not seen in the total voter's graph. This was because of Colorado House Bill-1303, Create the Voter Access and Modernized Elections Act, took effect in July. This changed the definition of an inactive voter. Before this bill, if you did not vote in two previous elections you became an inactive voter. After the bill, only if USPS returned your ballot you became an inactive voter. If you received your ballot but did not vote for whatever reason you are still labeled as an active voter. This definitional change of what an active voter is does not affect my analysis but did come into play when trying to forecast future voter registration numbers. Another interesting point is the movement in registration numbers right before and after primary elections. Before the primary elections, roughly 6,000 voters change their registration status from Unaffiliated to either Democratic or Republican so they may vote in the party primaries. Then after the primary, they switchback. Forturenly, this does not need to happen anymore because of Proposition 108 passed in 2016, allowing independents to vote in primaries.

Now that I understand my data a little more it was time to do some heavy analysis. Two common forms of time series analysis are moving averages and percent change from the previous month. Moving averages are common in financial forecasting because it helps reduce volatility in the data by smoothing out the graph. While this useful in other time series problems I did not think it would help me out in analyzing this data because I am looking for different points of volatility in the data.

![change_month](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\change_month_1.png)

Percent change from the previous month was a graph I knew would be interesting to look at because these calculations do two interesting things. One, it will tell me if a particular political party is increasing or decreasing in numbers. And two, it automatically normalizes the data for me so I can comfortably compare the political parties to one another. Above is the percent change from the previous month graph over the whole entire data set for active voters by party. In this graph, I am most interested in times when these lines separate from each other. This would mean that a political party is either increasing or decreasing its amount of active voters at a different rate than the other two. The first two sections I notice were the huge spike in 2013 and the few months around primary elections. Both of these were explained earlier in this blog. This graph with one little addition will allow me to answer my question about political momentum I talked about above.

To answer my original question of if political momentum can be quantified in the number of registered voters for a political party, I used the percent change from the previous month graph above, with a couple of other features. These features included limiting the graphs to only the odd years and adding a label to the graphs that allowed me to only look at counties that were won by either the democrat or the republican. For example, in the year 2017, I was able to look at counties that voted in favor of Donald Trump or in favor of Hillary Clinton. Below is what these two graphs look like side by side.

![change_winner](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\change_by_winner.png)

First, looking at the graph on the above, the counties that voted for Donald Trump, you can see a clear separation in the Republican(red) and the Democrats(blue) line. The red line hovers in positive numbers, 0.0 to .002, meaning that the number of Republican registered voters is always growing throughout this time frame. While the number of registered Democrats are almost always in the negative numbers, meaning that the number of registered Democrats is almost always decreasing through this time frame. Now, compare this to the graph on the left, the counties that voted for Hillary Clinton, both the red and blue lines move almost together until September. Next, take a look at the unaffiliated(purple) line. This line has as a steeper slope for the counties that voted for Trump than voted for Clinton. It also has a higher apex as well. This means that in counties that voted for Trump, voters that registered as unaffiliated rose at a greater rate than in the counties that voted for Clinton.

In the 2016 election, the presidential race is defiantly considered the top ticket race on the ballot, while in 2014 the gubernatorial race is considered the top of the ticket race on the ballot. In the 2014 gubernatorial race between Bob Beauprez(R) and John Hickenlooper(D) you can see the same trends mentioned above for this race as well. That graph can be seen here. This pattern is not as extreme but it still is there. Due to Colorado House Bill-1303, I was not able to complete this analysis for the 2012 presidential election.

But with that being said, I want to look at this from a statewide election point of view, Clinton won Colorado and Hickenlooper won his race as well. So, if a county in Colorado was in favor of the losing candidate for the top of the ticket statewide race, you will see an increase of registered voters for the losing party. But, counties that voted for the winning candidate did not see the same spike in registered voters for the winning party. For example, as we saw above, counties that voted for Trump, who lost Colorado, saw an increase in Republican registered voters but, in counties that voted for Clinton, we did not see the spike in registered Democrat voters.

If I was apart of a political party and using the information, it could possibly be worth it run targeted register to vote campaigns during the first few months of a new year while the previous elections are still fresh in peoples minds. For example, in Colorado, the Republican party could run a register to vote campaign in counties that voted for Walker Stapleton(The losing candidate in the gubernatorial race). Or the Democratic party could run a register to vote campaign in counties in Texas that voted for Beto O’Rourke (The losing candidate in the Senate race in Texas). I know those go against what I said earlier that gubernatorial races have a bigger impact than Senate races but given who was in the race and the national spotlight that was shined during the election I think it is safe to say that this was the biggest race during the previous Texas election. I am also looking at the race from another state so take it with a grain of salt.

The last part of this project was to forecast out voter registrations for the year 2018. With the seasonality as mentioned above, I thought a Seasonal ARIMA model would work well with my data set. ARIMA stands for “Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average.” The formula for this model is below.

![arima](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\arima.png)

Not only does this model look at periods before the predicted time but it will also look at periods from the season or seasons before as well to help with its forecasting. In this case, each period was one month and each season was two years or 24 periods.

![forecast](..\assets\images\political_momentum_images\forecast.png)

To the eye, these forecasts may look reasonable but I do not trust these forecasts as much as I would like. There are a few reasons for this but they all come back to is that I do not have enough data for my forecast to be correct. With the data, I have available to me, this is a problem I can not solve either. The definition of an inactive and active voter changed in 2013 as mentioned above does not allow me to go back any farther than I already do.

My model can only look back one season if I include any autoregressive and moving average components. This is a problem because of the massive influx of voters in 2016 due to the controversial presidential election. Looking at Novembers in even years for the grand total of registered voters to goes: 2012–3.65 million, 2014–3.66 million and 2016–3.87 million. My forecast predicts November of 2018 to have 4.02 million voters. My predictions predict a very large for a non-presidential year. This is one flaw that I see in my model. If I was able to look back three or even 4 seasons It would help mitigate the large increase in 2016 and better to be able to tell the difference between a presidential year and a non-presidential year to produce better results.

Looking at the actual total number of registered voters from November of 2018 my prediction was not as far off as I thought it would be. That number was 3.99 million. While I was able to convince myself that my model would not be that accurate due to logical reasons sometimes it is better to wait for the real-life data before coming to a conclusion. To be fair I still think that number would be more accurate if I had a few more years of viable data.
